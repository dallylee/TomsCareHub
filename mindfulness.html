Finding Calm and Strength

A practical guide to mindfulness and emotional steadiness during your cancer treatment.

Ommmmmm

This guide is about giving you some simple, practical tools to help find a bit of steadiness in the middle of it all. It’s just some science-based practical advice about helping you manage the anxiety, improve your sleep, and handle the day-to-day stresses of treatment, so you can feel more in control.

With focus on small, science-backed steps you can take.

What is Mindfulness (and what it isn't)

Mindfulness is simply paying attention to the present moment, on purpose, without judging it.

  • It IS noticing your breath.
  • It IS noticing the feeling of your feet on the floor.
  • It IS noticing a worried thought, seeing it ("Ah, there's that worry again"), and letting it go by, like a cloud.
  • It IS NOT about "clearing your mind" or "stopping thoughts." (That's impossible!)
  • It IS NOT a religion.
  • It IS NOT a competition. There is no "failing" at mindfulness.

Why Your Mind Matters for Treatment

Your mind and body are constantly talking to each other. When you're under high stress, your body produces more "stress hormones" like cortisol. This can:

  • Disrupt your sleep.
  • Make you feel more fatigued.
  • Increase your perception of pain.
  • Worsen feelings of nausea.

By using these simple techniques to calm your nervous system, you can help dial down that stress response. This can make a real, physical difference to your quality of life, helping you sleep better and tolerate treatment more easily.

Your Core Practices Toolkit

These are the foundational techniques. Don't try to master them. Just try them. Five minutes is a perfect start.

Practice: Controlled Breathing

This is your "emergency brake" for anxiety. It directly stimulates the vagus nerve, telling your body it's safe to relax.

How to do "Box Breathing":

  1. Sit comfortably in a chair, feet on the floor.
  2. Slowly exhale all the air from your lungs.
  3. Gently inhale through your nose for a count of 4.
  4. Hold the breath gently for a count of 4.
  5. Slowly exhale through your mouth for a count of 4.
  6. Hold the empty breath for a count of 4.
  7. Repeat 5-10 times.

Tip:

If holding the breath feels stressful, just focus on a long, slow exhale. Try inhaling for 4 and exhaling for 6. The slow exhale is the most important part for relaxation.

Practice: The Body Scan

This practice helps you reconnect with your body and notice tension you might be holding. It's excellent for doing in bed before sleep.

How to do a 5-Minute Body Scan:

  1. Lie down or sit comfortably. Close your eyes.
  2. Bring your attention to your feet. Just notice them. Are they warm? Cold? Tingly? No need to change anything, just notice.
  3. Slowly move your attention up to your lower legs. Then your knees. Then your thighs. Spend 15-20 seconds on each part.
  4. Move your attention to your stomach, your lower back, your chest.
  5. Move it to your hands. Your arms. Your shoulders. (This is a common place to hold tension - just notice it, and on an exhale, imagine it softening).
  6. Finally, bring your attention to your neck, your jaw, and your face. Soften your forehead.

Tip:

Your mind *will* wander. That's what minds do. When you notice it's wandered off, just gently and kindly bring your attention back to the part of the body you were on. It's not a failure, it's part of the practice.

Practice: Mindful Movement (Gentle Yoga)

Fatigue can make exercise feel impossible, but gentle movement can actually *give* you energy. This is about listening to your body, not pushing it.

How to do a "Chair Cat-Cow":

  1. Sit in a chair with your feet flat on the floor and your hands on your knees.
  2. Inhale: Gently arch your back, look up slightly, and roll your shoulders back. (This is "cow").
  3. Exhale: Slowly round your spine, tuck your chin to your chest, and let your shoulders come forward. (This is "cat").
  4. Move slowly with your breath. Repeat 5-8 times.

How to do a "Mindful Walk":

Just walk, even if it's just to the end of your street. Leave your phone in your pocket. Pay attention:

  • Notice the feeling of your feet hitting the pavement.
  • Notice the feeling of the air on your skin.
  • Notice 3 things you can see, and 3 things you can hear.

Safety Note:

Listen to your body. If you feel dizzy or sharp pain, stop. Be extra gentle around any port lines or surgical sites. "Gentle" is the key word.

Practice: Gratitude & Journalling

This isn't about pretending everything is fine. It's about training your brain to notice the small, good things that still happen, even on hard days. This can shift your mood and perspective.

How to do it:

  • The 3 Good Things: Before sleep, get a notebook. Write down three small, good things that happened. They can be tiny: "The tea tasted good this morning." "A friend sent a funny text." "I saw a dog out the window."
  • Expressive Writing: If you feel overwhelmed, try "dumping" your thoughts onto a page for 10 minutes. Don't worry about grammar or spelling. Just write whatever is in your head. Afterwards, you can even tear the page up. It's just a way to get the thoughts *out* of your head.

Tip:

This can feel silly at first. Stick with it for three days. You might be surprised at what you start to notice.

In-the-Moment Help (Your Toolkit)

These are specific "drills" for high-stress moments. Click on a topic to see a simple plan.

Use the "5-4-3-2-1" Grounding Technique

This pulls your mind out of the future (worry) and into the present (safety). Right now, where you are, pause and name:

  • 5 things you can SEE (the computer, a crack in the ceiling, your hand).
  • 4 things you can FEEL (your feet on the floor, the chair under you, your shirt).
  • 3 things you can HEAR (the fridge humming, a car outside, your own breath).
  • 2 things you can SMELL (soap on your hands, coffee).
  • 1 thing you can TASTE (or just the inside of your mouth).

Take a deep breath. You are here, right now.

Acknowledge, Anchor, Act

  1. Acknowledge: Say to yourself, "I am feeling anxious. That makes sense." Don't fight the feeling. Just name it.
  2. Anchor: Do 10 "Box Breaths" or "4-6 Breaths." Place a hand on your chest or stomach and feel it rise and fall. Anchor yourself in your body.
  3. Act: Do a simple, distracting *task*. Don't just watch TV. Tidy a drawer. Do a puzzle. Listen to a specific album. Engage your mind in a simple, concrete task.

Create a "Calm Bubble"

The chemo ward can be busy. You're allowed to create your own quiet space.

  • Headphones are key: Prepare a playlist of calming music, an audiobook, or guided meditations (see 'Resources' tab).
  • Comforts: Bring a soft blanket, warm socks, a flask of your favourite tea.
  • Focus: Instead of watching the drip, close your eyes and do a Body Scan. Or, practice "Mindful Eating" with the snack they give you. Pay 100% attention to the taste and texture.

Don't Fight It. Get Up.

Lying in bed worrying about not sleeping is the worst thing you can do. It teaches your brain that "bed = stress."

  1. The 20-Minute Rule: If you're awake for more than 20 minutes, get out of bed.
  2. Go to another room: Keep the lights dim.
  3. Do something boring: Read a dull book (no phones!). Do a Body Scan. Listen to a boring podcast.
  4. Return to bed: When you feel sleepy again, go back to bed.

This is a core part of CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia) and it works.

The Science & Where to Go Next

The Science Corner: Does this really work?

Yes. This isn't "alternative" medicine; it's now a standard part of supportive cancer care, recommended by Macmillan and the NHS. Large studies (meta-analyses) on mindfulness for cancer patients have shown:

  • Significant reductions in anxiety, depression, and stress (often 30-40% improvement in scores).
  • Improved sleep quality and less reliance on sleep medication.
  • Reductions in fatigue, which is one of the most common and difficult side effects.
  • Better pain tolerance and a lower "perception" of pain.
  • It may even have a small, positive effect on immune markers and inflammation (by reducing stress hormones).

Resources for Your Journey (UK)

  • NHS Mindfulness: Your GP can often refer you to free or low-cost online mindfulness (MBCT) courses.
  • Macmillan Cancer Support: Their website has a wealth of guided audio, videos, and resources. They also fund mindfulness sessions at many centres.
  • Maggie's Centres: These centres (often near big hospitals) are amazing. They offer free drop-in mindfulness, yoga, and psychological support. Look up your nearest one.
  • Apps (Free versions):
    • Insight Timer: Huge library of free guided meditations.

For You & Your Clinical Team

This section has summaries you can share with your doctor, nurse, or psychologist.

When to Pause and Seek Help

These practices are very safe, but it's important to get professional help if you're struggling. This is not a replacement for psychological therapy.

If you feel: Persistent low mood, lack of interest in anything, or feelings of hopelessness for more than a week.
The right action is: Talk to your GP or Clinical Nurse Specialist. This could be depression, and it's treatable.
If you feel: Overwhelming anxiety, panic attacks, or find that mindfulness makes you feel *more* anxious or agitated.
The right action is: Pause. Talk to your GP or ask for a referral to a hospital psychologist. You may need trauma-sensitive or one-on-one support.
If you feel: Any sharp pain, dizziness, or breathlessness during mindful movement or yoga.
The right action is: Stop immediately. Talk to your physiotherapist or team.

Clinician Appendix: Key Evidence

  • MBSR/MBCT for Cancer Patients (Meta-Analysis): A 2020 meta-analysis (Cillessen et al., Psycho-Oncology) of 29 RCTs found mindfulness-based interventions had significant positive effects on reducing anxiety, depression, and stress, and improving quality of life. Effects were largest for psychological distress. (DOI: 10.1002/pon.5430)
  • Yoga for Cancer-Related Fatigue (Meta-Analysis): A 2018 review (Zeng et al., Integrative Cancer Therapies) of 13 RCTs found yoga significantly reduced cancer-related fatigue compared to control groups, with effects durable at follow-up. (DOI: 10.1177/1534735418758830)
  • CBT-I for Insomnia in Cancer: CBT-I is considered the first-line treatment for chronic insomnia. In cancer populations, it shows robust effects on sleep onset, maintenance, and quality, and is recommended by NCCN and ASCO guidelines over pharmacology.
  • Safety & Adherence: Adherence to 8-week programmes is moderate-to-high. Primary safety concern is in patients with severe, unmanaged PTSD, where mindfulness may require adaptation (trauma-informed approach). Physical movement (yoga) requires adaptation for port lines, neuropathy, and bone stability.
