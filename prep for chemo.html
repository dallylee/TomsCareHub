Your Chemotherapy Prep Guide

Hi Tom. Let's walk through this.

Receiving a diagnosis and a treatment plan can feel like a whirlwind. It's completely normal to feel overwhelmed. This guide is designed to cut through the noise and give you clear, practical steps you can take over the next two weeks.

The goal isn't to bombard you with medical jargon. It's to help you feel more prepared, more in control, and to set up systems at home that will make the coming weeks easier. Think of it as your personal project plan. You're not just a passenger here; you're an active part of your own care team.

We'll cover what to pack, questions to ask, simple things you can do to help with side effects, and what to do if you feel unwell. You don't have to do it all at once. Just one step at a time.

You've got this. And we've got a plan.

Your 14-Day Action Plan

Your Focus for the Next 2 Weeks

Your main job right now is preparation, not reaction. We want to get everything in place *before* you need it, so you're not scrambling later. This means getting prescriptions, stocking up on supplies, and creating a 'comfort station' at home.

Your Pre-Chemo Checklist

  • Set Up Your Symptom Diary: Get a simple notebook or use a phone app. Plan to jot down 1-10 scores for things like fatigue, nausea, or appetite each day. This log will be *gold* for your nurses to help you feel better, faster.
  • Get Your 'Red Flag' Numbers: Program these into your phone and put them on the fridge:
    • Your Clinical Nurse Specialist (CNS) daytime number.
    • The 24/7 Chemo Hotline or Acute Oncology number.
    • Your GP's number.
  • Ask About 'Anticipatory Prescriptions': Talk to your team about getting prescriptions *now* for common side effects, especially anti-sickness and anti-diarrhoea meds (like Loperamide). Having them in your cabinet is much better than needing them at 2 AM.
  • Assemble Your Mouth Care Kit: Chemo can make your mouth sensitive. Get these ready:
    • A *soft* or baby-sized toothbrush.
    • Mild, non-mint, alcohol-free mouthwash (e.g., salt water or a prescribed one).
    • A mild, non-whitening toothpaste (like a children's one).
  • Plan Your Chemo Day Bag: Pack a bag with comfort items: a blanket, warm socks, a water bottle, snacks, a book or tablet (with charger), and headphones.
  • Do a Safety Check (Interactions): Go to the "Safety Net" tab in this guide. Read the list of interactions *today* and stop taking anything on it, especially St. John's Wort. Make a list of *all* other vitamins, herbs, or pills you take (even occasionally) and show it to your pharmacist or nurse.
  • Ask Your Team for Your Vitamin D Level: Ask for a simple blood test to check your Vitamin D. It's a very common deficiency in the UK, and getting your levels right is a good foundation for treatment. We cover this in the "Daily Toolkit" tab.

Side-Effect Prep: Your 'How-To' Guides

You might not know your exact chemo plan yet, and that's okay. Different drugs have different side effects. Once you know which drugs you'll be on, you can click below to get a specific plan. If you don't know, just ask your nurse: "Will my plan include Oxaliplatin, Irinotecan, or Capecitabine?"

Plan for: Nerve Sensitivity (Neuropathy) & Cold

Oxaliplatin can make your nerves (especially in your hands, feet, and throat) very sensitive, particularly to cold. This often starts right away.

What to do:

  • AVOID COLD: This is your main job. For the first 3-5 days after your infusion:
    • Drink only room-temperature or warm drinks. No ice, no cold-from-the-fridge drinks.
    • Don't eat ice cream or ice lollies.
    • Wear gloves when reaching into the fridge or freezer.
    • Wrap up warmly (scarf, gloves) if you go out in cold or windy weather.
  • IMPORTANT: NO ICE CHIPS: Because of this cold sensitivity, you should *not* use ice chips (oral cryotherapy) during your infusion, as it can trigger throat spasms or discomfort.
  • Track It: Tell your team *at every visit* if you have any tingling, numbness, or "pins and needles." They can adjust your dose to help prevent it from becoming permanent.
  • If it becomes painful later: Studies show the drug Duloxetine can help. This is something to discuss with your team if needed, but the main thing for now is prevention by avoiding cold.

Plan for: Diarrhoea & Tummy Upset

Irinotecan can cause diarrhoea, and it's very important to manage it quickly so you don't get dehydrated.

What to do:

  • Get Loperamide (Imodium): Ask your team for the 'chemo diarrhoea' dosing rules. It's often a different (higher) dose than the one on the box. Have it ready at home.
  • Call Your Team: Do not 'wait and see'. Call your 24/7 hotline if you have diarrhoea, especially if it's watery, frequent, or happens at night. This is a "Red Flag" (see Safety Net tab).
  • Hydrate: Keep drinking plenty of fluids (at room temperature) to replace what you're losing.
  • CRITICAL INTERACTION: AVOID GRAPEFRUIT: Do not eat grapefruit or drink grapefruit juice. It seriously interferes with how your body processes Irinotecan and can make side effects much worse.

Plan for: Mouth Care & Skin Care

These drugs (5-FU infusion or Capecitabine tablets) can affect the fast-growing cells in your mouth and on your hands and feet.

1. Mouth Care (Oral Cryotherapy)

For bolus 5-FU infusions (a fast "push" of the drug), sucking on ice chips can protect your mouth.

  • Who is this for? Only for patients getting a 5-FU *bolus* (push) infusion.
  • Who is this NOT for? Do NOT do this if you are also getting Oxaliplatin (see the Oxaliplatin tab).
  • The Protocol: Ask your nurse for ice chips. Start sucking on them 5 minutes *before* your 5-FU bolus starts. Continue sucking and swirling the cold water in your mouth for a total of 30 minutes.

2. Skin Care (Hand-Foot Syndrome)

This can cause redness, soreness, or peeling on your palms and soles. Prevention is key.

  • Get Your Skin Kit Ready:
    • A thick, simple moisturiser with Urea (e.g., Eucerin 10% Urea).
    • Soft cotton gloves and socks (to wear after moisturising).
    • A gentle, non-soap cleanser.
  • Be Gentle: Avoid hot water, harsh chemicals (like washing-up liquid - wear gloves!), and friction (like from tight shoes or jarring exercise) on your hands and feet.
  • Moisturise: Apply the urea cream generously to hands and feet at least twice a day, starting from Day 1.

Your Daily Toolkit: Body & Mind

These are small, positive actions you can control every day. They are evidence-based to help with fatigue, mood, and well-being during treatment. Start building these habits now.

Your Daily Nutrition Plan

  • Ask for a Vitamin D Test: As mentioned in the checklist! Research (like the SUNSHINE trial) suggests that having good Vitamin D levels might be helpful for people with mCRC. This is *not* about taking mega-doses on your own, but about having your doctor check your level and prescribe the right dose *for you* to get you into a healthy range.
  • Aim for Protein: Protein helps your body repair itself. Try to include a source with each meal (e.g., chicken, fish, eggs, dairy, beans, lentils, tofu).
  • Hydration is Key: Aim for 2 litres (8-10 glasses) of fluid a day. Water, squash, or herbal teas are all good. This helps flush the chemo drugs out and reduces side effects.
  • Food Safety: Your immune system might be lower at times. Be extra careful with food hygiene: wash hands, separate raw/cooked foods, cook food thoroughly, and be wary of high-risk items (e.g., unpasteurised dairy, raw eggs, pre-prepped salads).

Your Daily Mind & Body Routine

  • 10-Min Mindfulness: Major cancer guidelines (like ASCO/SIO) recommend mindfulness to help with anxiety and fatigue. Just 10 minutes a day can make a difference. Try a free app like 'Headspace' (they often have free cancer content), 'Calm', or 'Insight Timer'.
  • 20-30 Min Walk: This is huge. Guidelines recommend aiming for 150 minutes of moderate exercise (like a brisk walk) per week. A 20-30 minute walk most days hits this target. On low-energy days, a 10-minute gentle stroll still counts. It's one of the best-proven ways to fight treatment-related fatigue.
  • Sleep Window: Try to go to bed and wake up at roughly the same time, even on weekends. This strengthens your body clock and can improve sleep quality and energy.
  • 2-Line Mood Check-in: In your symptom diary, add two lines: "How's my mood (1-10)?" and "One good thing today was...". It's a simple way to stay aware of your mental health.

Your Weekly Movement Goal

A 20-30 minute walk most days helps you hit the 150-minute weekly goal recommended by experts to help reduce fatigue.

High-Risk Interactions: What to Avoid

Some foods, herbs, and supplements can dangerously interfere with chemotherapy, either making it too strong (more side effects) or too weak (less effective). When in doubt, don't take it.

Item to AVOID Which Chemo? Why? (The Simple Version) What to do
St. John's Wort ALL Chemo Speeds up your body's "clean-up" enzymes, which can clear the chemo from your system too fast, making it less effective. STOP taking it immediately. Tell your doctor.
Grapefruit & Seville Oranges Especially IRINOTECAN (and others) It *blocks* the "clean-up" enzymes, causing a dangerous build-up of the chemo drug, leading to severe side effects. AVOID completely (juice and fruit).
High-Dose Antioxidants ALL Chemo (on infusion days) Some chemo works by creating 'oxidative stress' to harm cancer cells. High-dose vitamins (like C or E) *might* interfere with this. Avoid high-dose supplement pills. Eating fruit and veg is perfectly fine and healthy.
Other Herbs / Supplements ALL Chemo Many herbs (e.g., turmeric pills, green tea extract) can interact. The risk is unknown. Show your *entire* list of pills to your pharmacist. Do not start anything new.
