Eating for Strength and Steadiness

A practical nutrition guide to support you through chemotherapy.

Liži pičku

An Opening Note

So here I planned to just tell you to eat Greek tomato, because Jesus did. But instead here’s a little guide to give you practical, evidence-based ideas on how nutrition can support you over the next few weeks and months.

This guide focuses on using food to help you tolerate treatment, maintain your strength, manage side effects, and support your overall wellbeing. It's based on the best human evidence I could find specifically for colorectal cancer.

Most importantly: This information is to support you alongside your medical care, not replace it. Your NHS oncology team, specialist dietitian, and pharmacist are your primary source of advice. Please talk to them before starting any new supplements or making big dietary changes.

What Matters Most: A Checklist

If you're feeling overwhelmed, just focus on these key things:

  • ✓ Stay nourished and hydrated. Your body needs fuel and water to cope with treatment. Aim for small, regular meals and sip drinks throughout the day. Try to avoid unintended weight loss.
  • ✓ Prioritise protein. Protein is vital for maintaining muscle and repairing tissues. Aim to include a source (like fish, chicken, eggs, dairy, beans, or lentils) with every meal and snack.
  • ✓ Be guided by your symptoms. Some days will be harder than others. On bad days, "fed is best." Eat what you can tolerate. On good days, aim for more nourishing, fibre-rich foods. We have a whole section for this.
  • ✓ Practice food safety. When your immune system is low (neutropenia), it's extra important to handle food safely. Wash hands, cook food thoroughly, and be careful with high-risk items.
  • ✓ Talk to your team. Keep your doctors and nurses updated on any side effects, especially severe diarrhoea, constipation, or poor appetite. Ask for a referral to an NHS dietitian.

Questions to Ask Your Team

Jot down your questions and take them to your next appointment:

  • Can I be referred to a specialist oncology dietitian?
  • My appetite is low and I'm losing weight. What support is available? (e.g., prescription sip feeds)
  • Can you check my Vitamin D level?
  • I'm thinking of taking [supplement name]. Is this safe to take with my specific chemotherapy (FOLFOX/CAPOX/FOLFIRI)?
  • I'm struggling with [symptom name]. What else can I try?

When to Pause or Seek Advice

This guide is for general support, but some symptoms need urgent medical attention. Contact your oncology helpline or NHS 111 if you experience:

  • A high temperature (fever) during chemotherapy – this could be neutropenic sepsis.
  • Severe, uncontrolled diarrhoea (e.g., more than 6-8 times a day) or vomiting.
  • Inability to keep any fluids down for more than 12-24 hours.
  • No bowel movement for 3 or more days, despite trying laxatives.
  • Sudden shortness of breath or chest pain.
  • Extreme fatigue, dizziness, or confusion.

Symptom Playbook

Chemotherapy can cause side effects. How you eat can make a real difference in managing them. Here are strategies for the most common issues. Choose what feels right for you today.

Managing Nausea & Vomiting

  • Eat small and often. An empty stomach can make nausea worse. Try 5-6 small meals or snacks instead of 3 large ones.
  • Go for "plain and dry." Try crackers, toast, rich tea biscuits, or plain rice. These are often well-tolerated.
  • Sip fluids slowly. Don't gulp. Try sipping water, weak squash, or clear broths. Some people find ginger tea or flat ginger ale helpful.
  • Choose cold foods. Cold or room-temperature foods often have less smell than hot foods, which can be off-putting. Try a chicken sandwich, yoghurt, or fruit.
  • Avoid triggers. Steer clear of greasy, fried, spicy, or very sweet foods for now.
  • Tell your team. Don't just "put up with it." Your team can prescribe very effective anti-sickness medications. Take them as prescribed, often *before* you start to feel sick.

Managing Diarrhoea

  • Hydrate and replace salts. This is the #1 priority. Sip water, weak squash, or rehydration drinks (like Dioralyte). Broth or soup can help replace lost salts.
  • Go "low and white." For a few days, switch to low-fibre, "low-residue" foods. Think white bread, white rice, white pasta, potatoes (no skin), plain chicken, fish, and eggs.
  • Try the "BRAT" foods. Bananas, white Rice, Applesauce (stewed apple), and dry Toast are gentle on the gut.
  • Limit triggers. Avoid greasy/fried foods, spicy foods, caffeine, alcohol, and very high-fibre foods (like raw veg, skins, pips, and whole grains) until things settle.
  • Re-introduce fibre slowly. Once the diarrhoea has stopped, gradually re-introduce fibre-rich foods over several days.
  • Tell your team. Severe diarrhoea (e.g., 6+ times a day) needs to be reported, especially with FOLFIRI, as it can be serious.

Managing Constipation

  • Hydrate well. Fluids help to soften stools. Aim for 8-10 glasses of fluid a day, unless you've been told otherwise. Warm drinks in the morning can help.
  • Gently increase fibre. If you're eating well, slowly increase your intake of soluble fibre (from oats, pulses, fruits) and insoluble fibre (from whole grains, nuts, veg).
  • Try "P" foods. Prunes, pears, and pulses can all help get things moving. Start with a small amount.
  • Get moving, if you can. Gentle activity, even just a short walk, can help stimulate your bowel.
  • Talk to your team early. Constipation is a very common side effect of chemotherapy and pain relief. Your team can prescribe safe and effective laxatives. Don't wait until it's been several days.

Managing a Sore Mouth (Mucositis)

  • Choose soft, moist foods. Think soups, stews, scrambled eggs, mashed potatoes, yoghurt, ice cream, or smoothies. Add gravy or sauces to moisten food.
  • Avoid "sharp" foods. Stay away from anything hard, crunchy, or acidic. This includes crisps, crusty bread, raw vegetables, and citrus fruits (oranges, lemons, tomatoes).
  • Go for cool or warm, not hot. Very hot foods can be painful. Let food cool down. Sucking on ice chips can be soothing.
  • Use a straw. Sipping drinks or smoothies through a straw can help bypass sore areas.
  • Practice good mouth care. Use the mouthwash your team provides. Use a very soft toothbrush. Rinse your mouth with a salt or bicarbonate of soda solution after eating.
  • Glutamine? There is some evidence that L-glutamine powder swished in the mouth may help. (See the Supplements section and discuss with your team).

Managing Taste Changes

  • If food tastes bland: Add flavour with herbs, spices (if your mouth isn't sore), garlic, or ginger. Marinades can help. A little salt or sugar can sometimes lift flavours.
  • If food tastes metallic: This is very common with 5-FU. Try using plastic or wooden cutlery instead of metal. Drink from a glass or mug, not a can.
  • Try tart flavours. A little lemon, lime, or vinegar (if your mouth is okay) can help cut through a metallic taste. Sucking on sugar-free lemon sweets can help.
  • Go for cold foods. Cold chicken, salads, or sandwiches may have less of a metallic taste than hot meals.
  • Try new foods. Sometimes the foods you usually love taste the worst. Be open to trying different things for a while.
  • Zinc? There's low-to-moderate evidence that zinc supplements *might* help with taste changes. (See the Supplements section and ask your team).

Managing Fatigue & Low Appetite

  • Eat small and often. The "little and often" rule is key. Aim for a snack or small meal every 2-3 hours.
  • Make every bite count. Choose "nutrient-dense" foods. This means foods with lots of calories and protein in a small package.
    • - Add butter, olive oil, or cream to potatoes or soups.
    • - Add grated cheese to meals.
    • - Snack on nuts, seeds, or peanut butter (if you can).
    • - Use full-fat milk and yoghurt.
  • Have snacks ready. Keep easy-to-grab snacks visible: a bowl of nuts, cheese and crackers, pots of yoghurt, fruit.
  • Try nourishing drinks. If eating feels too much, sip on smoothies, milkshakes, or soups. Your team can also prescribe high-calorie "sip feeds."
  • Accept help. Let friends and family cook for you or prepare snacks. A freezer full of easy-to-heat meals is a huge help.
  • Gentle movement. It sounds counter-intuitive, but a very short, gentle walk can sometimes help boost energy levels and stimulate appetite.

Building Your Core Plate

On days you feel well, focusing on balanced meals can help you build and maintain strength. This section is about the "how-to" of eating well, including your personal protein goals and practical meal ideas.

Your Personal Protein Target

Protein is your body's main building block, crucial for healing and keeping your muscles strong during treatment. The target for people with cancer is higher than for the general public, typically 1.2 to 1.5 grams of protein per kilogram of your body weight per day.

How to Hit Your Target

The best way is to spread your protein intake across the day. Aim for 20-30g of protein at each main meal, plus protein in your snacks.

  • A 100g chicken breast has ~30g protein.
  • A 100g salmon fillet has ~22g protein.
  • 2 large eggs have ~13g protein.
  • A pot of Greek yoghurt (170g) has ~17g protein.
  • A tin of lentils (drained) has ~18g protein.

Hydration is Key

Staying hydrated is vital. It helps your kidneys flush out the chemotherapy drugs and can manage side effects like fatigue and constipation. Aim for 8-10 glasses (about 2 litres) of fluids a day.

All fluids count:

  • Water, squash, herbal teas, milk.
  • Soup, broth, and jelly also count.
  • If you have diarrhoea, you will need more fluids and salts (see Symptom Playbook).

A Simple Shopping List

Stock your kitchen with easy, nourishing options:

  • Protein Power: Tinned fish (tuna, salmon), eggs, full-fat Greek yoghurt, cottage cheese, pre-cooked chicken, tofu, tinned beans and lentils.
  • Easy Carbs: Oats (porridge), white rice, white bread, crackers (for nausea), wholemeal bread (for good days), potatoes.
  • Fluids: Herbal teas (peppermint, ginger), squash, fruit juice (to mix with water), milk, soup.
  • Symptom Helpers: Bananas, applesauce, dry biscuits, ice lollies.
  • Flavour: Peanut butter, jam, herbs, spices.

Exploring Foods & Dietary Patterns

When you're feeling well, you might wonder about the bigger picture: what style of eating is best? And are there any "superfoods"? Here's what the evidence says. The key is progress, not perfection.

Dietary Patterns: The Big Picture

Mediterranean & Prudent Patterns

This is where the strongest evidence lies. A "Mediterranean" or "Prudent" diet is not a strict diet, but a pattern rich in vegetables, fruits, whole grains, beans, nuts, and fish. It's low in red/processed meats and sugary snacks. This pattern is linked to better health outcomes and may help support your body's resilience.

Plant-Forward & Fibre

You don't need to be vegetarian, but aiming for a "plant-forward" plate is a great goal. This means filling at least half your plate with plant foods (veg, fruit, whole grains, pulses). Fibre from these foods is crucial for a healthy gut microbiome, which is very important in colon cancer.

However: If you have diarrhoea or a blockage, you will need to follow your team's advice on a low-fibre diet temporarily.

Fasting, TRE, and Keto Diets

You may hear a lot about these. Here's the current clinical view:

  • Fasting/Fasting-Mimicking: Some early human trials suggest short-term fasting *during* chemo *might* reduce side effects. However, the evidence is not yet strong, and it's not suitable for everyone. CRITICAL: Do not attempt fasting without close supervision from your oncology team. Weight loss is a major risk.
  • Time-Restricted Eating (TRE): This means eating within an 8-10 hour window (e.g., 10am-6pm). This is easier than fasting and has some general health benefits. It may be safe to try, *but only if* you can still eat enough food to maintain your weight.
  • Ketogenic (Keto) Diets: Very low-carb, high-fat diets. There is no strong human evidence that keto diets improve cancer outcomes, and they can be very hard to follow and may worsen weight/muscle loss. Not generally recommended during treatment.

Alcohol

It's generally best to avoid alcohol during chemotherapy. It can interact with your drugs, worsen side effects like a sore mouth, and puts extra strain on your liver (which is already working hard). If you do choose to have a drink, please discuss it with your doctor first.

Specific Foods with Good Signals

No single food is a miracle cure, but some have good evidence for supporting general health and a healthy gut.

Oily Fish (Omega-3)

Salmon, mackerel, herring, and sardines. Rich in EPA and DHA, which may help reduce inflammation and maintain weight. Aim for 1-2 portions a week.

Whole Grains, Nuts, Seeds

Oats, brown rice, pulses, and nuts are packed with fibre that feeds your good gut bacteria (prebiotics). Great on "good" days.

Crucifers & Alliums

Broccoli, cauliflower, kale, as well as onions, garlic, and leeks. These contain compounds studied for their protective effects. Cook them well if you feel bloated.

Mushrooms

Many types of mushrooms (even simple button mushrooms) contain unique fibres like beta-glucans, which can support the immune system. Cook well.

Fermented Foods

Live yoghurt, kefir, and sauerkraut contain beneficial bacteria (probiotics). They can be a good way to support your gut microbiome.

Coffee & Tea

Good news for coffee lovers. Evidence in CRC survivors suggests moderate coffee intake (2-4 cups) is safe and possibly beneficial. Green tea is also rich in antioxidants.

A Guide to Supplements

This is one of the most confusing topics. Many supplements are marketed to patients, but few have strong human evidence of benefit during chemotherapy, and some can be harmful. Always tell your oncology pharmacist about any supplement you take.

CRITICAL: Safety First - Interactions & Cautions

Some supplements and foods can seriously interfere with your chemotherapy, making it less effective or increasing side effects. The chemo regimens for colon cancer (like FOLFOX, CAPOX, FOLFIRI) are sensitive.

  • DO NOT TAKE ST. JOHN'S WORT. It can speed up the breakdown of many chemo drugs (especially irinotecan), making your treatment less effective.
  • AVOID GRAPEFRUIT & SEVILLE ORANGES. This includes the juice. They can block the breakdown of some drugs (like irinotecan), leading to dangerously high levels in your body.
  • BEWARE HIGH-DOSE ANTIOXIDANTS. Avoid high-dose "cocktails" of vitamins A, C, E, or selenium *during* chemotherapy, as they *might* interfere with how chemo works. A standard multivitamin is usually fine, but ask your team.
  • CHECK BLOOD THINNERS. If you take warfarin or other anticoagulants, be cautious. High-dose fish oil, turmeric (curcumin), and vitamin E can increase bleeding risk.
  • FOLATE & 5-FU: Your chemo (5-FU) works by blocking folate. Do not take high-dose folic acid supplements unless specifically told to by your oncologist. The folate (folinic acid) given *with* your chemo is part of the treatment.

Common Myths vs. Facts

Myth: "Sugar feeds cancer."

Fact: All your cells, including healthy ones, use sugar (glucose) for energy. While it's smart to avoid *excess* sugar from sweets and sugary drinks, completely cutting out all sugar/carbs is not proven to help and can lead to dangerous weight loss.

Myth: "An alkaline diet can cure cancer."

Fact: This is a persistent myth with no scientific basis. You cannot change your body's blood pH with food. The diet is harmless (it encourages fruit/veg) but has no special anti-cancer effect.

Myth: "I need a 'detox' to help my liver."

Fact: Your liver is the detox organ; it doesn't need "detoxing" by special juices or supplements. In fact, many "detox" supplements are unregulated and can be harmful to the liver. The best way to support your liver is to avoid alcohol and eat a balanced diet as you are able.

Appendix for Your Clinical Team

Key Guidelines & Evidence Summary

  • Protein & Energy: Evidence consistently supports enhanced energy (25-30 kcal/kg/day) and protein (1.2-1.5 g/kg/day) targets to prevent/treat cachexia and maintain muscle mass during treatment. (Ref: ESPEN, ASCO Guidelines). Weight maintenance is a key prognostic factor.
  • Dietary Patterns: Strong cohort data supports Mediterranean and "Prudent" (high fruit/veg/whole grain) patterns for CRC survivors. Evidence for keto/fasting during chemo is preliminary (small RCTs) and carries significant risk of weight loss; not recommended as standard of care.
  • Fibre & Microbiome: High fibre intake is robustly associated with improved CRC survival. This must be balanced with low-residue diets for acute symptoms (diarrhoea, obstruction). Probiotic use (esp. LGG) has moderate backing for reducing incidence/severity of chemo-related diarrhoea.
  • Omega-3 (EPA/DHA): Moderate evidence from multiple RCTs supports using ~2g/day of EPA+DHA for inflammation modulation and as part of a multimodal strategy to attenuate cachexia. Requires monitoring with anticoagulants.
  • Vitamin D: High prevalence of deficiency in cancer patients. Correction is 'High' evidence for skeletal health. Association with CRC outcomes is strong but intervention data is still emerging. Testing is recommended.
  • Folate & 5-FU: High-dose folic acid supplementation is contraindicated as it may reduce 5-FU efficacy. Folinic acid (leucovorin) given *with* 5-FU is therapeutic, acting to stabilise the drug-enzyme complex.
  • Drug-Nutrient Interactions (High Evidence):
    • - St. John's Wort: Potent CYP3A4 inducer, significantly reduces irinotecan exposure. Avoid.
    • - Grapefruit/Seville Orange: Potent UGT1A1 & CYP3A4 inhibitor, significantly increases irinotecan/5-FU toxicity. Avoid.
  • Liver Considerations: No evidence supports "detox" diets. In case of liver mets or mild hepatotoxicity, the goal is safe, adequate nutrition. Avoid unvetted herbal supplements which may be hepatotoxic.
