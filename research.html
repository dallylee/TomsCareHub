Key Practice-Relevant Headlines

  • KRAS G12D Breakthrough: New targeted therapy combinations for the previously "undruggable" KRAS G12D mutation (the most common KRAS type in colon cancer) showed a significant benefit in Phase 3 trials, moving quickly toward UK regulatory filing.
  • Treatment De-escalation: Large trials confirmed that using circulating tumour DNA (ctDNA) blood tests after surgery allows many patients to safely skip or reduce chemotherapy, dramatically improving quality of life.
  • HER2 ADCs Mature: Antibody-Drug Conjugates (ADCs) targeting the HER2 protein solidified their role, especially in colon cancer patients who are HER2-positive and need later-line options.
  • TIGIT Disappointment: Highly anticipated combination immune therapies using the new TIGIT checkpoint unfortunately showed no significant added benefit in major Phase 3 trials, leading to a focus shift on other immunotherapy partners.
  • Intratumoural Promise: Early Phase 2 data suggests that injecting oncolytic viruses directly into tumours could 'wake up' the immune system, potentially making resistant tumours (like the most common type of colon cancer) responsive to existing immunotherapy.

How to Read Trial News, Without the Headache

Trial reports often use jargon. Here are a few essential terms to know, so you can judge the news for yourself:

  • Progression-Free Survival (PFS): The time a patient lives without their cancer getting worse. This is a common and important goal (or endpoint) in trials. An improvement in PFS means the drug is controlling the cancer for longer.
  • Hazard Ratio (HR): This is the best way to see the *relative* benefit. An HR of 0.50 means the treatment group had a 50% lower chance of the event (like progression or death) compared to the control group. The closer the number is to zero, the better the drug is performing.
  • Line of Therapy: This refers to *when* a treatment is used. First-line is the initial therapy; Second-line is what follows the first. Trials often test drugs in specific lines.
  • Absolute Benefit: The *actual* difference in time or percentage. For example, if a control group's PFS is 4 months and the new drug's PFS is 8 months, the absolute benefit is 4 months. This number is often more meaningful than the Hazard Ratio.
"Look for the absolute difference and ensure the Hazard Ratio is well below 1.0. This tells the real story."

Tom, given your diagnosis of colon cancer with liver metastases, the key focus areas for you are biomarker testing, KRAS treatment, and ctDNA guidance. Here is a summary of the most relevant 2025 developments.

Targeted Therapy Focus: The KRAS G12D Revolution

The biggest news this year is for KRAS mutations. Colon cancer often has one of the several KRAS subtypes, with G12D being the most common and historically the hardest to treat. The new combination therapy entering review (see Card 1 in the previous tab) showed it can finally meaningfully control this subtype, marking a true paradigm shift for patients who test positive for it.

Action: Your tumour tissue should be tested for all KRAS subtypes (G12C, G12D, etc.) to ensure future options are known.

Immunotherapy: Expanding Options Beyond MSI-H

You may have heard of MSI-H/dMMR tumours, which respond exceptionally well to standard immunotherapy. While most colon cancers are MSS (Microsatellite Stable) and don't respond, 2025 brought hope for this group:

  • MSS Drug Combinations: Trials combining targeted agents with immunotherapy are still working to make MSS tumours responsive.
  • New Delivery Methods: The promising oncolytic virus trials (Card 4) are specifically exciting for MSS CRC, aiming to transform the tumour environment so that current immunotherapy drugs can finally work.

Insight: If you are MSS, the focus is on combinations and innovative delivery methods via clinical trials.

Access, Myths, and Next Steps in the UK

Understanding how to access these new options in the NHS is as important as understanding the science.

UK Access and Eligibility

The pathway to accessing new drugs usually involves the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (NICE) or clinical trials.

  • Genomic Testing: All relevant metastatic cancer patients on the NHS are entitled to comprehensive genomic testing (to check for KRAS, HER2, etc.). Ask your oncologist to confirm yours has been done or is planned.
  • NICE Approval: Once drugs like the new KRAS inhibitor get regulatory approval, they must be assessed by NICE. This process can take 6-12 months before they are routinely available across the NHS.
  • Trial Search: Your best immediate access to Phase 2/3 drugs is through clinical trials. Ask your oncologist about trials running locally or through national networks like the NCRI (National Cancer Research Institute).
  • Second Opinions: The NHS supports patients seeking a second opinion, especially when discussing eligibility for complex trials.

Myths and Media Hype (Sanity Check)

The media loves a good headline. Itâ€™s vital to be clear about uncertainty and avoid over-interpretation.

  • Avoid "Cure" Language: Medical science rarely uses this word. Focus on words like "control," "response," and "survival extension."
  • Subgroup Fishing: If a drug only seems to work well in a *small subset* of the trial (e.g., only those over 70), this is often unreliable. Always ask if the main trial endpoint was met for the whole group.
  • Preclinical Data: If a study is in Phase 1 or only tested on mice (preclinical), it means it's a very long way from being available. It's exciting, but not an immediate option.
  • Negative is Good: Trials that show a drug *doesn't* work (like the TIGIT example) are hugely important. They prevent people from having unnecessary side effects and steer research toward more promising areas.

What to Ask at My Next Appointment

These five questions are tailored to help you discuss the latest research and biomarker testing:

  • "Can we confirm the complete genomic testing of my tumour, specifically for KRAS (all subtypes), HER2, and MSI/dMMR status?"
  • "Given my current treatment plan, if I progress, what are my options for the new KRAS G12D inhibitor combination?"
  • "Are there any clinical trials open at this centre or in the region for my specific cancer type that involve ctDNA-guided therapy or novel combinations?"
  • "If I respond well to treatment, what role could ctDNA monitoring play in safely managing my maintenance therapy or taking a treatment break?"
  • "Is there any reason (like my liver metastases or other health factors) that would make me immediately ineligible for immunotherapy or ADCs later on?"

Pivotal Negative Trials (Guiding Research Focus)

1. Anti-TIGIT Combination (e.g., Tiragolumab/Atezolizumab):

Major Phase 3 trials in first-line NSCLC (PD-L1 positive) demonstrated no statistically significant improvement in OS or PFS when adding the anti-TIGIT agent to PD-1 blockade, compared to PD-1 blockade alone. This has dampened enthusiasm for the TIGIT axis as a general-purpose checkpoint inhibitor partner.

2. KRAS G12C Inhibitor (2nd Generation) Monotherapy:

Confirmatory Phase 3 trials showed that KRAS G12C monotherapy did not meet its primary OS endpoint in mCRC (Phase 3). While active, the absolute benefit requires combination partners to be clinically compelling and standard of care.

CRC Specific Trial Schema and Eligibility Quirks

  • ctDNA Monitoring: The Phase 3 studies on de-escalation strictly defined "ctDNA negative" as two consecutive negative readings, often separated by 4 weeks, using a personalised, tumour-informed assay. Commercial assays vary in sensitivity and are not interchangeable for trial-guided decisions.
  • Liver-Directed Therapy + Systemic: There is an increased focus on trials combining SBRT or ablation for oligometastatic liver disease with systemic IO or Targeted Agents. Eligibility often requires strict control of extra-hepatic disease and specific baseline liver function thresholds.
  • Autoimmune Exclusion (IO/ADC): It is crucial to ensure Tom's history is reviewed for any underlying autoimmune conditions or prior severe immune-related adverse events, as this remains a frequent exclusion criterion for I-O and certain ADC trials.
