Thinking about Alternatives

A practical guide exploring complementary and alternative options; from getting legally high to weird diets and more

A Cautious Welcome

A Friendly but Critical Disclaimer

The internet is full of "miracle cures" and strong opinions, which is confusing and overwhelming. This guide is designed to be a calm, balanced map of that world. It looks at options *beyond* standard care, from what patients themselves discuss, to what integrative clinics are using. Your single most important rule is: Talk to your oncology team or pharmacist before starting *anything* new.

Options by Your Goal

You're probably not looking for a specific supplement, but for something to help a specific problem. Here are common options, grouped by what people use them for. This is just information, not a recommendation.

Medical Cannabis (CBD & THC)

Evidence: Moderate Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Mixed Interaction: Possible

What it's used for: This is a major topic. Patients report using medical cannabis (which has both THC and CBD) for nausea, to stimulate appetite, for pain, and to help with sleep and anxiety. Evidence is moderate but growing.

How it works: THC (the psychoactive part) is known to reduce nausea and boost appetite. CBD (non-psychoactive) is studied for anxiety, inflammation, and pain.

Practical Notes: In the UK, this is only legally available via private prescription from a specialist clinic (it's not usually prescribed on the NHS for this). Products are oils or flowers (vaporised). Patients often report that a "balanced" oil (e.g., 10mg THC:10mg CBD) is more effective for symptoms with fewer side effects than high-THC alone.

Safety & Interaction: This is the key part. Cannabis (especially CBD) can interact with CYP450 enzymes in your liver, which also process your chemo drugs. This *could* alter the levels of irinotecan or oxaliplatin in your body. It can also cause sedation or dizziness. You MUST discuss this with your team. Do not drive. Unregulated "street" oils (like RSO) are a huge risk - you don't know the dose or if they contain contaminants.

Acupuncture

Evidence: High (for nausea) Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Favourable Interaction: Low

What it's used for: Strong evidence shows acupuncture can help manage chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting. Many patient-support hubs (like Macmillan) recommend it.

How it works: It's thought to stimulate specific nerves, which send signals to the brain to release anti-nausea and pain-relieving chemicals.

Safety & Interaction: Very safe when done by a qualified practitioner. Find someone registered with the British Acupuncture Council. The main caution is to let them know if you are neutropenic (low white blood cells) or have a low platelet count, as there's a small risk of infection or bleeding. Tell them you have liver metastases so they avoid deep needling in that area.

Yoga, Meditation & Breathwork

Evidence: High Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Favourable Interaction: Low

What it's used for: High-quality evidence shows these practices can significantly reduce anxiety, improve sleep quality, and help manage the fatigue and "chemo-brain" that come with treatment.

How it works: They activate the "rest and digest" (parasympathetic) nervous system, calming the "fight or flight" response that is often in overdrive during a cancer diagnosis.

Safety & Interaction: Extremely safe. The key is to be gentle. Look for "gentle yoga," "restorative yoga," or "yoga for cancer" classes. Many cancer centres offer them for free. There are also great apps like "Calm" or "Headspace."

Melatonin

Evidence: Moderate Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Favourable (short-term) Interaction: Possible

What it's used for: To help reset a broken sleep cycle (insomnia), which is very common during treatment.

How it works: It's a hormone your brain makes naturally to signal sleep. A supplement can help "kick-start" that process.

Safety & Interaction: Generally very safe for short-term use at low doses (e.g., 2mg). In the UK, it's prescription-only. It can interact with blood pressure drugs or anticoagulants, so it's important to let your team know you're considering it.

Acupuncture (for Neuropathy)

Evidence: Moderate Real-world Signal: Moderate Safety: Favourable Interaction: Low

What it's used for: Oxaliplatin (the 'OX' in FOLFOX/CAPOX) can cause painful peripheral neuropathy (tingling, pain, numbness in hands and feet). Some studies suggest acupuncture may help reduce the severity of this.

Safety & Interaction: As before, very safe with a qualified practitioner. It's one of the most-recommended integrative therapies alongside standard care. See cautions in the 'Nausea' section.

Boswellia (Frankincense)

Evidence: Low Real-world Signal: Moderate Safety: Mixed Interaction: Possible

What it's used for: A powerful anti-inflammatory. It's used in traditional medicine for joint pain and is being researched for inflammatory gut issues. Patients sometimes try it for general aches and pains.

Safety & Interaction: Evidence is still low. It can interact with CYP liver enzymes, similar to CBD, so it carries a *possible* interaction risk with your chemo. It's not a first-line choice and needs a discussion with your pharmacist.

Medicinal Mushrooms (e.g., Turkey Tail)

Evidence: Low (in UK) Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Mixed Interaction: Unknown

What it's used for: This is one of the most common supplements patients ask about. In Japan, a specific extract (PSK) is approved as an add-on to cancer treatment. Patients use it hoping to support their immune system during chemo.

How it works: They contain compounds called beta-glucans, which are thought to stimulate parts of the immune system.

Safety & Interaction: This is the problem. We have very little data on how these extracts interact with chemo like FOLFOX. Stimulating the immune system might not be wise during neutropenia. The products are also unregulated, so quality varies hugely. Most UK oncologists will advise caution and ask you to avoid them during active treatment.

Mistletoe (Viscum album)

Evidence: Low (for survival) Real-world Signal: Strong Safety: Mixed Interaction: Possible

What it's used for: Very common in mainland Europe (especially Germany), given as an injection. It's claimed to support quality of life and reduce chemo side effects.

How it works: It's thought to have immunomodulatory effects.

Safety & Interaction: Evidence for a survival benefit is very weak. Some quality-of-life studies are more positive. It must be prescribed by a specialist (e.g., an Anthroposophic doctor). It can cause flu-like symptoms. Because it affects the immune system, it's vital to discuss it with your oncologist. Not generally recommended by NHS teams.

Exploring Diet Patterns

You'll hear a lot about "cancer diets." Most are myths, but some are being seriously studied. Your main goal is to avoid weight loss, so any diet that makes you eat too little is a bad idea.

Plant-Forward Anti-Inflammatory

Evidence: High Safety: Favourable

What it is: This is the "Green Light" option. It's not a strict diet, but a pattern: filling your plate with vegetables, fruits, whole grains, and beans. It includes fish, yoghurt, and nuts, and is low in red/processed meat and sugary foods.

Verdict: This is the safest and most evidence-based pattern for general health and recovery. It supports your gut microbiome. Aim for this on days you feel well.

Short-Term Fasting

Evidence: Low (but growing) Safety: Mixed

What it is: Not eating (water only) for a set period (e.g., 24-72 hours) *around* your chemo infusion. The theory is it might "protect" healthy cells and make cancer cells more vulnerable.

The Evidence: A few small human trials have shown it *might* reduce side effects. Patient reports are mixed; some feel great, others feel awful. We have no strong data on survival.

Verdict: This is an "Amber Light" for a reason. The biggest risk for you is losing weight and muscle, which is dangerous. Do NOT attempt this without your oncologist's supervision. It's unsuitable for many people.

Ketogenic (Keto) Diet

Evidence: Low Safety: Concerning

What it is: A very high-fat, very low-carbohydrate diet. The theory is to "starve" cancer cells of glucose.

The Evidence: There is no strong human evidence that keto improves outcomes for colon cancer. It is *extremely* hard to follow, can cause constipation, and often leads to... losing weight and muscle.

Verdict: Most oncology dietitians do not recommend keto during chemo because the risks (weight loss, poor nutrition, feeling awful) often outweigh any theoretical benefit.

How to Try Things Safely

If you and your team agree to try something, here are some rules to follow to keep you safe.

Your Safety Checklist

1. Talk to Your Pharmacist First. This is the most important step. Your oncology pharmacist is the expert on interactions. Take the actual bottle (or a clear photo of the label) to your appointment so they can check the ingredients.

2. One Change at a Time. Don't start five new supplements at once. If you get a side effect (or feel better), you won't know what's causing it. Start one, give it a week or two (if your team agrees), and see how you feel.

3. Start Low, Go Slow. Don't start with a high dose. Begin with the lowest recommended dose and see how you tolerate it.

4. Check for Quality. The supplement world is poorly regulated. Look for products with "Third-Party Testing" or "GMP" (Good Manufacturing Practices) certification. For cannabis, only use legal, lab-tested products from a clinic.

5. Stop Before Surgery or Infusions. Many supplements (like fish oil, curcumin) can affect bleeding or interact with anaesthetics. The standard rule is to stop all supplements 1-2 weeks before any surgery. Ask your team when to stop/start around your chemo infusions.

6. Listen to Your Body. If something makes you feel nauseous, dizzy, or gives you a rash, stop taking it immediately and tell your team.

Red Flags & Myths to Park

If you see any of these, be very sceptical:

  • "Cures cancer." (Anything that claims this is a lie.)
  • "Secret formula." (Lack of transparency is a huge red flag.)
  • "Better than chemotherapy." (This is dangerous and preys on fear.)
  • "No side effects." (Everything that has an effect can have a side effect.)
  • "Big Pharma is hiding this." (Conspiracy theories are a sales tactic.)
  • "Alkaline" or "Detox" diets. (These are myths. Your body controls its own pH. Your liver and kidneys *are* your detox system.)

A Closing Note

It's completely understandable to want to feel more in control. Exploring these options is a way of doing that. The goal is to find things that *support* you, help you feel steady, and make treatment more tolerable, without adding new risks.

The best approach is an "integrative" one: combining the best of standard, evidence-based medicine with safe, supportive complementary therapies, all with the full knowledge of your excellent NHS team. You've got this.

Appendix for Your Clinical Team

Tom, this section has the clinical-facing summaries and references. You can show this to your oncologist, dietitian, or pharmacist to help your conversation.

Pharmacology & Interaction Notes (CRC Regimens)

  • St. John's Wort: Contraindicated. Potent P-gp and CYP3A4 inducer. Will significantly decrease plasma concentrations of Irinotecan (SN-38) and potentially 5-FU.
  • Grapefruit & Seville Orange: Contraindicated with Irinotecan. Potent inhibitor of UGT1A1 and CYP3A4, leading to increased SN-38 exposure and severe toxicity (diarrhoea, neutropenia).
  • Cannabis (CBD & THC): Use with caution. CBD is a known inhibitor of CYP3A4, CYP2C9, and UGTs. THC is a substrate. Potential for pharmacokinetic interaction with Irinotecan and 5-FU is high. Patient-reported real-world use is also high. Clinical monitoring is warranted.
  • Curcumin & Boswellia: Use with caution. Both interact with CYP enzymes and may have anti-platelet effects, posing a risk with anticoagulants or thrombocytopenia.
  • Green Tea Extract (EGCG): High-dose extracts are associated with hepatotoxicity. Given liver mets, this is a needless risk. Standard tea consumption is fine.
  • Fasting/Fasting-Mimicking Diets: Emerging data from small RCTs (e.g., in breast/gynae cancers) suggest potential for reduced toxicity. However, risk of cachexia and sarcopenia in an mCRC patient is high. Not recommended outside of a clinical trial.
  • Acupuncture: Strong evidence base for CINV (Chemo-Induced Nausea/Vomiting) per multiple systematic reviews (Cochrane) and ASCO/NCCN guideline inclusion. Evidence for CIPN (Chemo-Induced Peripheral Neuropathy) is moderate but growing. Standard safety precautions for neutropenia/thrombocytopenia apply.
